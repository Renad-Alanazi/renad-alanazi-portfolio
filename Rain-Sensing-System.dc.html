Automotive Rain-Sensing Control System

An automotive control system that detects rainfall and automatically drives the headlights and windshield wipers, adapting headlight brightness and wiper speed to rain intensity to improve driver visibility and safety.

Role
System architecture, electronics, prototyping, testing
Domain

01 — Project Overview

Automating two safety systems from a single rain measurement.

Drivers often react late to changing rain: switching on headlights and wipers manually, at the wrong speed, or not at all. This project closes that loop in hardware: an optical sensor measures rainfall on the windshield, and the controller drives both the headlights and wipers automatically, scaling each output to the measured intensity.

The system was built and validated on a benchtop windshield rig. My work covered the full stack: system architecture, the sensor and control electronics, the MOSFET and relay power stages, mechanical CAD, prototype construction, and the testing program that produced the results below.

Problem
Manual headlight/wiper control lags behind changing rainfall, reducing visibility.
Objective
Detect rain and drive both systems automatically, scaled to intensity, in real time.
Outcome
90% automatic activation at a ~1.9 s response, validated on a windshield rig.

03 — System Architecture

A sense → decide → drive loop on a 12 V domain.

The optical sensor feeds an analog rainfall signal to the Arduino, which classifies intensity and commands two output paths: a MOSFET stage for PWM headlight brightness, and a relay stage for the wiper motor. A 12 V battery powers the loads; the controller updates both outputs continuously as conditions change.

04 — Circuit Design

Controller, drivers, and protected output stages.

Rain sensor interface
Optical sensor conditioned to the Arduino ADC; threshold sets the intensity tiers.
Arduino control system
Reads the sensor and issues PWM and digital control to the output stages.
MOSFET driver stage
Logic-level MOSFETs switch the headlights; PWM sets brightness per rain tier.
Relay motor control
Relays switch the wiper motor and its direction, isolating the inductive load.
Headlight circuitry
Headlight LEDs driven from the 12 V rail through the MOSFET stage.

10 — Challenges & Improvements

Single-threshold detection limit

Sensor saturation during heavy rainfall highlighted a limitation of the single-threshold detection method and informed future calibration improvements; spanning the full intensity range cleanly will require multi-level or adaptive thresholds.

Switching noise & isolation

Relays and the wiper motor are noisy inductive loads. Keeping the motor path relay-isolated from the logic protected the controller from switching transients.

Response time vs. stability

A ~1.9 s average balanced fast reaction against false triggering; reacting too quickly to single drops caused output chatter.

Sensor placement

Where the sensor sat on the windshield relative to the wiper sweep strongly affected readings. Modeling the rig in CAD first kept the geometry workable.
