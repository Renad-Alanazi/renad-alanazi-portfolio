01  Overview

A self-contained safety wearable combining biosignal sensing and emergency alerting.

The Personal Alert Device (PAD) is a wrist-worn embedded system developed as a team capstone project at Boston University. It monitors the wearer's heart rate and provides a manual emergency-alert trigger, combining biosignal acquisition, local event logging, and multi-modal user feedback in a battery-powered package small enough to wear.

My contributions centered on the electronics and system integration: schematic capture, custom PCB design, hardware prototyping, and the testing and validation of the assembled device. The sections below trace the design from system architecture through PCB development, firmware integration, and bench validation.

02  System Architecture

One SoC coordinating sensing, alerting, logging, and power.

The system is organized around a Seeed Studio XIAO nRF52840, an ARM Cortex-M4F SoC with integrated Bluetooth Low Energy, acting as the central controller. A MAX30102 optical sensor provides heart-rate data over I²C, while a momentary push-button serves as the manual alert trigger.

Three discrete status LEDs (red / amber / green) and a piezo buzzer form the alert and feedback path; a microSD card provides non-volatile event logging; and a 1100 mAh lithium-ion cell powers the device with USB-C recharging. The schematic captures the full interconnect, including 330 Ω LED current limiting and a PTC-protected sensing branch.

03  PCB & Hardware Design

A custom two-layer board on a XIAO-shield footprint.

The electronics were implemented on a custom two-layer PCB designed in EasyEDA and fabricated through JLCPCB. The board breaks out the nRF52840 module's I/O to the sensor, indicator, audio, and storage subsystems while keeping the assembly small enough to wear.

Placement prioritized a compact indicator cluster (the three LEDs exit the enclosure as a single status bar) and short, direct routing between the MCU and the I²C sensor bus. Connectors and the USB-C port were positioned to align with the 3D-printed case.

04  Firmware & System Integration

Bringing sensing, alerting, and logging together on one pin map.

Firmware on the nRF52840 samples the heart-rate sensor over I²C, evaluates the alert state, and drives the indicator and audio outputs accordingly. A push-button press raises a manual emergency alert, annunciated through the buzzer and the red status LED, while routine operation is reflected on the amber and green indicators.

Alert and sensor events are written to the microSD card for later review. The core system-integration task was verifying that the I²C sensor, GPIO-driven indicators, audio output, and storage all coexisted on the nRF52840's limited I/O, and that the assembled board behaved as the schematic intended.

05  Testing & Validation

Validated against a structured test plan.

The assembled device was validated against a test plan covering power-up, supply-rail integrity, sensor communication, and the alert path. Continuity and rail checks confirmed correct assembly; the MAX30102 was verified to return live heart-rate data; and the alert path was exercised end-to-end. Results were captured in a formal test report alongside the device user manual.

06  Challenges & Tradeoffs

Wearable packaging

Fitting the indicator LEDs, buzzer, sensor, and storage around the nRF52840 on a two-layer board demanded careful placement and I/O allocation. The enclosure had to align the LED cluster, button, and USB-C port with the board.

Mixed-subsystem integration

An I²C sensor, GPIO indicators, an audio output, and a storage interface had to share one constrained SoC. Validating that they coexisted reliably, without bus or timing conflicts, drove the integration effort.

Scope: GPS deferred

A GPS-based location feature was scoped during planning but not carried into the final hardware prototype. It is documented here as planned future work rather than a delivered capability.
