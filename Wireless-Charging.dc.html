Project 02  /  Wireless Power Transfer

Wireless Charging System

An inductive power-transfer subsystem that recharges a wearable's battery through its sealed enclosure: coil design, power chain, and a 3D-printed dock, validated on the bench at 5.07 V / 0.487 A into the cell.

Role
Coil & power-chain design, mechanical integration, testing

01  Overview

Contactless charging for a sealed wearable.

The wireless charging system recharges the wearable's 1100 mAh battery through its enclosure using inductive power transfer, eliminating an exposed charging connector. Energy is coupled across a small air gap from a transmit coil in a dock to a receive coil on the device.

I developed this subsystem end-to-end, from the coils and power chain to the 3D-printed dock and the bench validation of power delivery into the battery. The sections below cover the transfer architecture, the coil and mechanical design, and the measured results.

02  Transfer Architecture

From wall power to a charging cell, with no contacts.

A wall adapter converts mains AC to 5 V DC, feeding a transmitter module over a USB-B cable. The transmitter drives a hand-wound copper coil, which couples magnetically across the air gap to a receive coil on the wearable.

A receiver module rectifies and conditions the recovered energy to charge the 1100 mAh lithium-ion cell. The diagram below shows the full transmit and receive chain.

03  Coil & Power Electronics

Hand-wound coils, magnet-registered for repeatable coupling.

The link uses hand-wound copper coils on both the transmit and receive sides. Permanent magnets were added around the coils to register the dock to the wearable, holding the two coils coaxial and at a fixed separation, the alignment that most strongly governs coupling and delivered power.

The transmit side is powered from a regulated 5 V source; the receive side conditions the induced voltage to a level suitable for charging the lithium-ion cell.

04  Mechanical Integration

A dock the wearable drops into, charging through plastic.

A dedicated charging dock was 3D-printed to house the transmit coil, the USB-B inlet, and the alignment magnets, forming a cradle the wearable seats into. On the device side, the receive coil and its mating magnet are integrated into the enclosure so charging occurs through the plastic, with no electrical contacts exposed.

This contactless approach keeps the worn device sealed (there is no open connector to admit moisture or wear) while the magnets make alignment repeatable rather than dependent on careful hand placement.

05  Testing & Validation

Power delivery confirmed at the cell.

Power transfer was validated on the bench with a digital multimeter. At the receiver output, the system delivered a measured 5.07 V at a 0.487 A charging current into the battery, approximately 2.47 W of delivered power. These measurements confirmed successful end-to-end wireless power transfer and active charging of the 1100 mAh cell.

These figures represent measured delivered power at the receiver. Transfer efficiency, operating frequency, coil spacing, and full charge time were not characterized in this build and are identified as the next steps for formal power-electronics characterization.

06  Engineering Challenges & Tradeoffs

Alignment & air gap

Inductive transfer through an enclosure is sensitive to coil alignment and separation; small offsets reduce coupling and delivered power. Magnet-based registration was introduced to make alignment repeatable rather than relying on hand placement.

Hand-wound coil variation

Hand-wound coils introduce variation in inductance and series resistance, trading manufacturing precision for rapid prototyping. The design accepts that variability to iterate quickly toward a working link.

Sealed vs. delivered power

Charging through plastic with no contacts keeps the wearable sealed, but adds an air gap that costs coupling. The result balances delivered power against a contactless interface that needs no exposed connector.
