01 — Overview

A traffic intersection, implemented in pure hardware.

Modern traffic controllers run on microcontrollers. This system reproduces the same behavior (sequenced main-road signals, a protected left-turn arrow, and a crosswalk) using only analog and digital building blocks: a 555-timer time base, an analog comparator bank, CMOS logic gates, flip-flops, and discrete LED power drivers.

My work spanned the full stack: timing-circuit design, the comparator and logic network, custom LED driver boards, the multi-rail battery power system, and the prototype build, debugging, and validation. The sections below document the system through its wiring and subsystem diagrams, then the fully integrated hardware.

03 — Circuit Design Highlights

The engineering behind the controller.

Four subsystems carry the core engineering of the build. The original design schematics below document the timing generator, the LED driver channel, the battery power system, and the sound-activated power control, each annotated with the component values used in the prototype.

Timing core

NE555 sawtooth ramp generator

The master time base is a 555 timer that charges a 1000 µF capacitor into a slow sawtooth ramping from 199 mV to 3.335 V. A trim network (1.5k / 2.7k / 1k with a 27k path) sets the cycle period from 48.4 s to 59.7 s, and every downstream signal phase is defined by where this ramp sits at a given instant.

Output stage

LM317 + IRF510 LED driver channel

Each colour channel regulates current with an LM317 (2.5 V for red and yellow, 2.8 V for green and the arrow) into a parallel 8 Ω load, switched low-side by an IRF510 from the controller's logic signal. A 470 µF reservoir is added on the yellow channel only, to shape its transition behaviour.

Power system

Li-ion to 5 V regulation and charge detector

Two paralleled LM2937-5.0 regulators convert the 7.4 V Li-ion pack to the 5 V logic bus. A comparator stage built around a 5.1 V Zener reference and a BC177B drives two indicator LEDs, red below 6.3 V and green above 7.1 V, giving an at-a-glance state-of-charge readout.

Control

Sound-activated ON/OFF control

An electret microphone and LM358 amplifier detect a clap and fire a 72 ms monostable pulse. That pulse clocks an SN7476 flip-flop wired as a 1-bit toggle, whose output switches BS170 / BS250 and IRF510 MOSFETs in series between the controller ground and the supply negative, latching the whole system on or off hands-free.

06 — Outcome

A complete intersection controller, with zero lines of code.

This project delivers the full behaviour of a microcontroller-based traffic controller using only analog and digital hardware, with zero microcontrollers and zero firmware. Every state and transition that software would normally handle is realized physically, in a timing generator, a comparator bank, and a combinational logic network that sequence the intersection.

Building the state machine in hardware exercised the full breadth of the discipline: analog circuit design for the timing and threshold stages, digital logic design for the decode network, power regulation across multiple rails, MOSFET driver design for the signal loads, and the system integration, multi-board interconnect, and rail-first debugging and validation needed to make it all run together reliably.

The result is a multi-subsystem build rather than a single board: separate power, control, and LED driver boards tied together over a shared 12-pin signal bus, feeding multiple signal heads for the main road, a protected turn arrow, and the crosswalk, all sequenced on a trimmer-set 48–60 second cycle and powered from a single rechargeable battery.
