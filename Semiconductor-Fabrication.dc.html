Semiconductor Fabrication & Photolithography

Cleanroom microfabrication of patterned test structures on silicon: a three-mask Ti/Au metallization process executed through photolithography, wet-etch pattern transfer, and optical inspection.

01  Fabrication Overview

Building micron-scale structures through a controlled process sequence.

The objective was to design and fabricate semiconductor test structures using standard cleanroom microfabrication, while building practical command of the unit processes that make up a manufacturing flow: wafer preparation, thin-film deposition, photolithography, pattern transfer, and inspection.

Work was carried out on oxidized silicon wafers metallized with a titanium adhesion layer and gold, patterned across three mask levels by photolithography and defined by wet-etch pattern transfer. My role spanned hands-on processing, photolithography tasks, inspection of the finished structures, and documentation of each step and its observed result.

02  Process Flow

Ten unit steps, grouped into four process phases.

A · Substrate prep
01
Wafer cleaning & preparation
Silicon wafers cleaned to remove organic and particulate contamination before any film growth.
02
Dielectric layer: SiO₂
An insulating silicon-dioxide layer provides isolation beneath the metal pattern.
B · Deposition
03
Metal deposition: Ti / Au
A thin titanium adhesion layer (≈75 Å) followed by gold forms the conductive film to be patterned.
C · Photolithography
04
Photoresist application
Resist spin-coated to a uniform film, then soft-baked to drive off solvent.
05
Mask alignment & UV exposure
Each mask level aligned to the prior layer, then exposed under UV to transfer the pattern into the resist.
06
Development
Exposed resist developed away to open windows in the resist mask.
D · Pattern transfer & QC
07
Gold etch: KI / I₂
Exposed gold removed in a potassium-iodide etchant, transferring the resist pattern into the Au film.
08
Titanium etch
The underlying Ti adhesion layer etched to fully clear the field between features.
09
Photoresist strip
Remaining resist stripped, leaving the bare patterned metal structures.
10
Inspection & metrology
Optical microscopy used to verify pattern fidelity, alignment, and etch completeness across the die array.
03 — Photolithography Deep Dive

Where the pattern is defined and where tolerance is won or lost.

Photolithography sets the geometry of every layer, so it governed the precision of the whole flow. The sequence (coat, bake, align, expose, develop) was repeated for each of the three mask levels, with layer-to-layer alignment as the critical control point: each mask had to register to the features already on the wafer for the structures to connect as designed.

01
Spin coat & soft bake
Uniform resist thickness controls exposure and resolution; the soft bake stabilises the film.
02
Align to prior layer
Overlay accuracy across three masks determines whether features land where the design intends.
03
UV exposure
Dose sets the developed edge; under- or over-exposure shifts critical dimensions.
04
Develop
Develop time trades feature fidelity against resist loss; timing was held consistent per layer.
04 — Wafer Inspection

Verifying pattern fidelity across the die array.

Optical inspection was performed after pattern transfer and resist strip to verify feature definition, alignment quality, and repeatability across the wafer. Multiple regions were examined under microscopy to confirm that the fabricated structures matched the intended layout and remained consistent across the die array.

05 — Manufacturing Challenges & Observations

Layer-to-layer alignment

With three mask levels, overlay error accumulates. Careful registration to existing features was the difference between connected structures and misplaced geometry, the single most demanding control in the flow.

Wet-etch undercut

Isotropic gold and titanium etches attack laterally as well as vertically, undercutting the resist. Etch time had to clear the field without widening features beyond tolerance.

Adhesion & film integrity

The thin Ti layer is what keeps gold bonded to the oxide; without it the metal lifts. Process order and clean interfaces were essential to a film that survived etch and strip.

Contamination & yield

Particles and residue translate directly into defective dies. Cleanroom discipline and consistent step timing were the practical levers on yield across the wafer.

06 — Results & Takeaways

A fully patterned wafer, and a working feel for process control.

The flow produced a wafer of repeated, metallized test structures with the resist cleanly stripped and patterns transferred across the die array, the intended outcome of a three-mask Ti/Au process.

More importantly, the work built a hands-on understanding of how unit processes couple: how lithography sets tolerance, how etch chemistry and timing transfer it, and how cleanliness and consistency drive yield. These are the same control questions that scale into volume semiconductor manufacturing.
